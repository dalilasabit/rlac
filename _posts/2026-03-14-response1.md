Computational text analysis allows researchers to examine patterns in large texts through visualizations. Tools such as Voyant Tools and R make it possible to transform textual data into graphs, charts, and other visual forms. However, visualizations cannot always show their meaning and context clearly on their own. Without the context and the theme of analysis both LLMs and human readers may misinterpret the meaning of a visualization.
For Assignment 1, I analyzed a corpus consisting of two novels from the Harry Potter series by J. K. Rowling as well as three fan fiction texts. For this Response, I will use visualizations I generated for Assignment 1 which include antonym frequency graphs and a heatmap of specific word distribution across texts. I then will ask LLM to interpret these visuals without context and observe how the interpretations change after I provide additional prompts.

## Part 1: Description of the Visualizations
Using Voyant Tools and R, I generated several visualizations that represent patterns in my corpus. Two visualizations display the frequency of antonyms appearing across the texts. Antonyms such as “win” and “los*” or “love” and “hate” can reflect thematic oppositions that are common in fantasy literature. Another visualization is a heatmap showing the distribution of those antonyms across different texts in the corpus.

After providing Gemini with the context, its interpretation became more specific. It connected the spike of the word “win” in _Harry Potter and the Goblet of Fire_ to the Triwizard Tournament, which is a central competition in the novel. It also suggested that the higher frequency of “love” in one of the fan fictions may reflect the tendency of fan fiction to focus more on interpersonal relationships and emotional development. In addition, Gemini interpreted the high frequency of “fear” in _Harry Potter and the Deathly Hallows_ as reflecting the darker tone of the final book.
However, this interpretation still shows the limitations of visualization-based analysis. Word frequencies alone cannot fully capture narrative meaning or context, and the LLM’s conclusions remain interpretive rather than definitive. This demonstrates that even with contextual prompts, visualizations require critical interpretation by the researcher.

## Part 4: Visualizations and the Problem of Misleading Interpretation
The results of this experiment relate to broader discussions about misleading visualizations. In the article _How People Actually Lie With Charts_ by Maxim Lisnic, the author explains that charts often look objective, but they can still lead to misleading conclusions. This usually happens not because the chart is wrong, but because viewers interpret patterns without enough context.
This idea can also be seen in my experiment with the LLM. The visualizations I generated correctly represented the data, but when the LLM interpreted them without context, it tried to build explanations based only on visible patterns. For example, it described some texts as more “competitive” or “emotional” based on the frequency of certain words. While these interpretations sounded reasonable, they were not necessarily supported by the actual narrative context of the texts.
In this sense, the LLM behaved similarly to a human reader who tries to interpret a chart without fully understanding the dataset or the method behind it. This shows how easy it is to draw conclusions from visual patterns even when important contextual information is missing.

## Conclusion
This experiment shows that visualizations from computational text analysis do not automatically explain themselves. Tools such as Voyant Tools and R make it easier to find patterns in large collections of texts, but those patterns still need careful interpretation. When the LLM analyzed the visuals without context, it correctly described the structure of the charts but made speculative conclusions about their meaning. After I added more information about the corpus, the interpretations became more relevant to the themes of the Harry Potter texts. However, even with context, word frequency alone cannot fully explain the meaning of literary texts. Overall, this exercise shows that visualizations are useful tools, but they must be explained clearly and supported with context. Without explanation, both humans and AI systems may misinterpret what the data actually represents.
