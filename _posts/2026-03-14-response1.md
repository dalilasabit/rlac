# How Context Shapes LLM Interpretations of Textual Visualizations

Computational text analysis helps researchers find patterns in large texts using visualizations. Tools like Voyant Tools and R can turn text data into graphs and charts. However, these visuals don’t always convey their meaning clearly. Both LLMs and human readers may misinterpret them without context and themes.

For Assignment 1, I analyzed two Harry Potter novels by J. K. Rowling and three fan fiction texts. In this Response, I will use the visualizations I created for Assignment 1. First, I’ll ask the LLM to interpret these visuals. Then, I’ll give more prompts to see if the interpretations change.

## Part 1: Description of the Visualizations

Words like “win” and “los*” or “love” and “hate” illustrate common oppositions in fantasy literature. Another visualization is a heatmap showing the distribution of these antonyms across different texts.

This shows that even with context, visualizations need critical interpretation by the researcher.

## Part 4: Visualizations and the Problem of Misleading Interpretation
The results of this experiment relate to broader discussions about misleading visualizations. In the article _How People Actually Lie With Charts_ by Maxim Lisnic, the author explains that charts often look objective, but they can still lead to misleading conclusions. This usually happens not because the chart is wrong, but because viewers interpret patterns without enough context.

This idea can also be seen in my experiment with the LLM. The visualizations I generated correctly represented the data, but when the LLM interpreted them without context, it tried to build explanations based only on visible patterns. For example, it described some texts as more “competitive” or “emotional” based on the frequency of certain words. While these interpretations sounded reasonable, they were not necessarily supported by the actual narrative context of the texts.

In this sense, the LLM behaved similarly to a human reader who tries to interpret a chart without fully understanding the dataset or the method behind it. This shows how easy it is to draw conclusions from visual patterns even when important contextual information is missing.

## Conclusion
This experiment demonstrates that visualizations from computational text analysis don’t explain themselves automatically. Tools like Voyant and R help find patterns in large text collections, but those patterns need careful interpretation. When the LLM analyzed the visuals without context, it correctly described the charts' structure but made speculative conclusions. After I added more information about the corpus, the interpretations aligned better with the themes of the Harry Potter texts. Still, even with context, word frequency alone can’t fully explain literary meaning. Overall, this experiment shows that visualizations are valuable tools, but they need clear explanations and context. Without this, both humans and AI may misinterpret the data.
