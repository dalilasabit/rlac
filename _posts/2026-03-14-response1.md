Computational text analysis allows researchers to examine patterns in large texts through visualizations. Tools such as Voyant Tools and R make it possible to transform textual data into graphs, charts, and other visual forms. However, visualizations cannot always show their meaning and context clearly on their own. Without the context and the theme of analysis both LLMs and human readers may misinterpret the meaning of a visualization.
For Assignment 1, I analyzed a corpus consisting of two novels from the Harry Potter series by J. K. Rowling as well as three fan fiction texts. For this Response, I will use visualizations I generated for Assignment 1 which include antonym frequency graphs and a heatmap of specific word distribution across texts. I then will ask LLM to interpret these visuals without context and observe how the interpretations change after I provide additional prompts.

Part 1: Description of the Visualizations
Using Voyant Tools and R, I generated several visualizations that represent patterns in my corpus. Two visualizations display the frequency of antonyms appearing across the texts. Antonyms such as “win” and “los*” or “love” and “hate” can reflect thematic oppositions that are common in fantasy literature. Another visualization is a heatmap showing the distribution of those antonyms across different texts in the corpus.

The LLM successfully identified the technical structure of the visualization and explained that the graphs represent relative word frequencies across different documents. It also recognized the wildcard notation (win*) and used the data to identify patterns such as the higher frequency of “win” compared to “loss” and “love” compared to “hate.” However, without information about the corpus, Gemini made speculative conclusions about themes such as competitiveness and emotional tone. Because the texts actually belong to the Harry Potter universe these patterns may reflect specific narrative events rather than thematic dominance. This demonstrates how visualizations can be misinterpreted when contextual information about the corpus is missing.

In this interpretation, Gemini correctly explained how the visualization functions, identifying the axes, color scale, and the relative dominance of certain words such as “fear” and “happy.” The model also attempted to infer the emotional “vibe” of each text based on word frequency patterns. However, LLM does not know what the columns represent or how the words function in the narrative context. Word frequency alone cannot capture the meaning or role of these words within the texts. This example shows that while tools like R can highlight patterns in textual data, meaningful interpretation still requires contextual knowledge about the corpus and the research question.
